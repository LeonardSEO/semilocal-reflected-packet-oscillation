# Round 2E: full-line convolution, bilateral Laplace inversion, and zero expansion

Independent transform reconstruction for the frozen reflected-packet project.

## 1. Scope and conventions

Use the frozen all-prime-power error measure based at \(x=1\):

\[
 dE=d\Psi-dx,
 \qquad
 d\Psi=\sum_{n\ge2}\Lambda(n)\delta_n.
\]

Under \(r=\log x\), the interaction-weighted, one-sided logarithmic error
distribution is

\[
 \boxed{
 d\nu_+(r)
 =
 \sum_{n\ge2}\frac{\Lambda(n)}{\sqrt n}\delta_{\log n}(r)
 -
 \mathbf1_{[0,\infty)}(r)e^{r/2}\,dr.
 }
\tag{1.1}
\]

It is a locally finite signed measure on the full line, supported in
\([0,\infty)\). There is no atom at \(r=0\): \(x=1\) is neither a prime
power nor an atom of Lebesgue measure.

Let \(K\in C_c^\infty(\mathbb R)\) satisfy

\[
 \operatorname{supp}K\subset[-1,1],
\]

and put

\[
 \boxed{
 H(z)=\int_{-1}^{1}K(u)e^{-zu}\,du.
 }
\tag{1.2}
\]

For the mirror packet generated by a real
\(h\in C_c^\infty([-1/2,1/2])\),

\[
 K=K_h=C_{h,\mathsf Jh},
\]

and

\[
 H(z)
 =
 \left(\int_{\mathbb R}h(x)e^{zx}\,dx\right)^2.
\tag{1.3}
\]

If \(h\) is even, then \(K\) and \(H\) are even.

## 2. Canonical full-line convolution

Define

\[
 \boxed{
 J(t)
 :=
 (K*\nu_+)(t)
 =
 \int_{[0,\infty)}K(t-r)\,d\nu_+(r).
 }
\tag{2.1}
\]

Explicitly,

\[
 \boxed{
 J(t)
 =
 \sum_{n\ge2}\frac{\Lambda(n)}{\sqrt n}K(t-\log n)
 -
 \int_0^\infty K(t-r)e^{r/2}\,dr.
 }
\tag{2.2}
\]

For every fixed \(t\), both terms have a compact \(r\)-window, so (2.2)
requires no summation regularization. Convolution of a locally finite
measure with a compactly supported smooth function gives

\[
 J\in C^\infty(\mathbb R).
\]

Because \(\operatorname{supp}\nu_+\subset[0,\infty)\),

\[
 \boxed{\operatorname{supp}J\subset[-1,\infty).}
\tag{2.3}
\]

In particular, the canonical extension satisfies

\[
 J(t)=0\qquad(t<-1).
\tag{2.4}
\]

At \(t=-1\), the only possible contact is \(r=0\); there is no atom there
and a \(C_c^\infty\) kernel is flat at its support endpoint. Hence
\(J(-1)=0\), with the analogous statement for every derivative.

For \(t=2y>1\), the convolution window is

\[
 r\in[t-1,t+1]\subset(0,\infty).
\]

Consequently it agrees exactly with the original multiplicative
interaction:

\[
 \boxed{
 J(2y)
 =
 \int_0^\infty
 x^{-1/2}K(2y-\log x)\,dE(x),
 \qquad y>\frac12.
 }
\tag{2.5}
\]

## 3. Endpoint and extension audit

The basepoint \(r=0\) is load-bearing. The cumulative error based at zero in
the \(x\)-variable is

\[
 E_0(x)=\psi(x)-x,
 \qquad E_0(1)=-1.
\]

If one cuts \(E_0\) off at \(x=1\) and differentiates it, its jump creates
a spurious \(-\delta_1\), equivalently a spurious \(-\delta_0\) after taking
logs. The correct endpoint-normalized primitive is

\[
 E_1(x)=\psi(x)-(x-1)=E_0(x)+1,
 \qquad E_1(1)=0.
\]

It yields (1.1) with no boundary atom. Equivalently,

\[
 d\!\left(\mathbf1_{[1,\infty)}E_0\right)
 =
 \mathbf1_{[1,\infty)}dE-\delta_1,
\]

whereas

\[
 d\!\left(\mathbf1_{[1,\infty)}E_1\right)
 =
 \mathbf1_{[1,\infty)}dE.
\]

The physical value on \(t>1\) is independent of how the logarithmic
distribution is extended to \(r<0\). Indeed, if two extensions differ by
a distribution \(W\) supported in \((-\infty,0]\), then

\[
 \operatorname{supp}(K*W)
 \subset(-\infty,1],
\]

so

\[
 (K*W)|_{(1,\infty)}=0.
\tag{3.1}
\]

Thus (2.5), and hence every value with \(y>1/2\), is extension-independent.

The bilateral transform itself is not extension-independent. For example,
adding on \(t<-1\)

\[
 f_a(t)=e^{a(t+1)}\mathbf1_{(-\infty,-1]}(t),
 \qquad a>\frac12,
\]

does not change any value at \(t>1\), but in the common strip
\(\frac12<\operatorname{Re}z<a\) it changes the bilateral Laplace transform
by

\[
 \int_{-\infty}^{-1}e^{-zt}f_a(t)\,dt
 =
 \frac{e^z}{a-z}.
\tag{3.2}
\]

Therefore a full-line transform or its poles cannot be called canonical
until the zero extension (1.1)--(2.4), or an equivalent support convention,
has been fixed. Extra transform terms whose inverse is supported in
\((-\infty,1]\) do not alter the physical \(t>1\) formula.

## 4. Exact bilateral Laplace transform

For

\[
 \operatorname{Re}z>\frac12,
\]

both variations of \(e^{-zr}d\nu_+(r)\) have finite mass, and

\[
\begin{aligned}
 D(z)
 &:=
 \int_{[0,\infty)}e^{-zr}\,d\nu_+(r)\\
 &=
 \sum_{n\ge2}\frac{\Lambda(n)}{n^{z+1/2}}
 -
 \int_0^\infty e^{-(z-1/2)r}\,dr.
\end{aligned}
\]

Thus

\[
 \boxed{
 D(z)
 =
 -\frac{\zeta'}{\zeta}\!\left(z+\frac12\right)
 -
 \frac1{z-\frac12},
 \qquad \operatorname{Re}z>\frac12.
 }
\tag{4.1}
\]

The pole at \(z=1/2\) is removable in the meromorphic continuation: the
\(\zeta\)-pole contributes \(+(z-1/2)^{-1}\) to
\(-\zeta'/\zeta(z+1/2)\), and the continuous main term cancels it exactly.
In particular, there is no \(e^{t/2}\) residue in the error convolution.

The canonical convolution has the bilateral transform

\[
 \boxed{
 \int_{\mathbb R}e^{-zt}J(t)\,dt
 =
 H(z)D(z),
 \qquad \operatorname{Re}z>\frac12.
 }
\tag{4.2}
\]

This follows from Fubini after writing \(t=u+r\). Absolute convergence is
quantified by

\[
\begin{aligned}
 \int_{\mathbb R}e^{-ct}|J(t)|\,dt
 \le{}&
 \left(\int_{-1}^{1}|K(u)|e^{-cu}\,du\right)\\
 &\times
 \left(
 \sum_{n\ge2}\frac{\Lambda(n)}{n^{c+1/2}}
 +
 \frac1{c-1/2}
 \right),
\end{aligned}
\tag{4.3}
\]

for every \(c>1/2\).

There is no corresponding bilateral transform if one retains the
continuous error on the whole logarithmic line. Its negative-\(r\) part
requires \(\operatorname{Re}z<1/2\), while its positive-\(r\) part requires
\(\operatorname{Re}z>1/2\). This is the transform-level reason the Mellin
basepoint \(x=1\), or \(r=0\), is necessary.

## 5. Distributionally rigorous inverse formula

Fix \(c>1/2\). On the vertical line \(c+i\mathbb R\),

\[
 D(c+i\tau)
\]

and all of its \(\tau\)-derivatives are bounded, because its differentiated
Dirichlet series are absolutely convergent.
Moreover, for every \(N\),

\[
 |H(c+i\tau)|
 \le C_{K,c,N}(1+|\tau|)^{-N},
\tag{5.1}
\]

by repeated integration by parts. Hence \(H(c+i\tau)D(c+i\tau)\) is a
Schwartz function of \(\tau\).

Fourier inversion applied to \(e^{-ct}J(t)\) gives the exact identity

\[
 \boxed{
 J(t)
 =
 \frac1{2\pi i}
 \int_{c-i\infty}^{c+i\infty}
 e^{zt}H(z)
 \left[
 -\frac{\zeta'}{\zeta}\!\left(z+\frac12\right)
 -
 \frac1{z-\frac12}
 \right]dz.
 }
\tag{5.2}
\]

The integral is absolutely convergent, defines a \(C^\infty\) function,
and agrees with (2.1) both pointwise and in distributions. This is a
Bromwich formula in the genuine transform half-plane; no contour shift is
needed for (5.2).

## 6. Meromorphic data

Let \(\rho\) range over the distinct nontrivial zeros of \(\zeta\), and let
\(m_\rho\) denote the multiplicity of \(\rho\). The meromorphic continuation
of \(D\) has:

1. at
   \[
   z=\rho-\frac12,
   \]
   residue
   \[
   \boxed{-m_\rho;}
   \tag{6.1}
   \]

2. at every trivial zero \(s=-2n\), \(n\ge1\), equivalently
   \[
   z=-2n-\frac12,
   \]
   residue
   \[
   \boxed{-1;}
   \tag{6.2}
   \]

3. no pole at \(z=1/2\), by the cancellation in Section 4.

Therefore the exact residue coefficients for \(J\) are

\[
 \boxed{
 c_\rho[J]
 =
 -m_\rho H\!\left(\rho-\frac12\right),
 }
\tag{6.3}
\]

\[
 \boxed{
 c_n^{\rm triv}[J]
 =
 -H\!\left(-2n-\frac12\right).
 }
\tag{6.4}
\]

If \(H\) vanishes at one of these points, the corresponding pole is canceled
and its coefficient is zero.

For a real packet \(h\), formula (1.3) makes the coefficients completely
explicit:

\[
 c_\rho[J]
 =
 -m_\rho
 \left(
 \int h(x)e^{(\rho-1/2)x}\,dx
 \right)^2,
\tag{6.5}
\]

\[
 c_n^{\rm triv}[J]
 =
 -
 \left(
 \int h(x)e^{-(2n+1/2)x}\,dx
 \right)^2.
\tag{6.6}
\]

The trivial-zero factor in parentheses is real, so its square is
nonnegative. No sign assertion is made for an individual nontrivial-zero
coefficient; conjugate zeros combine to give a real contribution when
\(K\) is real.

## 7. Exact zero expansion for \(t>1\)

### Theorem

For every real \(t>1\),

\[
 \boxed{
\begin{aligned}
 J(t)
 ={}&
 -\sum_{\rho}
 m_\rho H\!\left(\rho-\frac12\right)
 e^{(\rho-1/2)t}\\
 &-
 \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}
 H\!\left(-2n-\frac12\right)
 e^{-(2n+1/2)t}.
\end{aligned}
 }
\tag{7.1}
\]

Both series, with every real derivative in \(t\), converge absolutely and
locally uniformly on \((1,\infty)\). No RH or simplicity of the zeros is
assumed.

When \(K\) is even, \(H\) is even. Reindexing the nontrivial-zero sum by the
functional-equation symmetry \(\rho\mapsto1-\rho\) then gives the equivalent
orientation

\[
 \sum_\rho
 m_\rho H\!\left(\rho-\frac12\right)e^{(\rho-1/2)t}
 =
 \sum_\rho
 m_\rho H\!\left(\rho-\frac12\right)e^{-(\rho-1/2)t}.
\tag{7.1a}
\]

Thus the sign of the translation exponent in an even-kernel presentation is
a reindexing convention; the residue coefficient and the algebraic minus
sign in (7.1) are invariant.

### Proof

Start with (5.2). Fix an integer \(R\ge2\) and shift the vertical line from
\(\operatorname{Re}z=c\) to \(\operatorname{Re}z=-R\). Choose horizontal
heights tending to infinity while staying a standard inverse-polynomial
distance from the zero ordinates. The classical zero-counting bound and the
logarithmic derivative estimate then give polynomial growth for \(D\) on
those horizontal segments. Estimate (5.1), uniformly on the fixed strip
\(-R\le\operatorname{Re}z\le c\), makes the horizontal integrals tend to
zero.

The crossed poles are all nontrivial-zero poles and the finitely many
trivial-zero poles with
\(-2n-\frac12>-R\). Their residues are (6.3)--(6.4).

On the left line, the functional equation and the absolutely convergent
Dirichlet series for \(\zeta'/\zeta(1-s)\) give

\[
 |D(-R+i\tau)|
 \le C\log(2+R+|\tau|)
\tag{7.2}
\]

for integer \(R\). Also, repeated integration by parts gives

\[
 |H(-R+i\tau)|
 \le
 C_{K,N}
 e^R(1+R)^N(1+|\tau|)^{-N}.
\tag{7.3}
\]

Consequently the remaining vertical integral satisfies, for every
\(t>1\),

\[
 \boxed{
 |\mathcal R_R(t)|
 \le
 C_{K,N}
 e^{-R(t-1)}
 (1+R)^{N+1}.
 }
\tag{7.4}
\]

It tends to zero as \(R\to\infty\). This proves (7.1), once convergence of
the residue series is checked.

For every \(A\) and every bounded real strip,

\[
 |H(\sigma+i\tau)|
 \le C_{K,A}(1+|\tau|)^{-A}.
\tag{7.5}
\]

Since the zero-counting function satisfies

\[
 N_\zeta(T)=O(T\log T),
\]

the nontrivial-zero series and its \(k\)-th \(t\)-derivative converge
absolutely. More precisely, for every \(k,M\ge0\), uniformly for \(t\) in a
fixed compact subset of \((1,\infty)\),

\[
 \boxed{
 \sum_{|\operatorname{Im}\rho|>T}
 m_\rho
 \left|
 \left(\rho-\frac12\right)^k
 H\!\left(\rho-\frac12\right)
 e^{(\rho-1/2)t}
 \right|
 =
 O_{K,k,M,t}(T^{-M}).
 }
\tag{7.6}
\]

For the trivial zeros,

\[
 |H(-x)|\le \|K\|_1e^x,
\qquad x>0.
\]

Thus, uniformly for \(t\ge1+\varepsilon\),

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
 &\sum_{n>N}
 \left(2n+\frac12\right)^k
 \left|
 H\!\left(-2n-\frac12\right)
 e^{-(2n+1/2)t}
 \right|\\
 &\hspace{25mm}
 \le
 C_{K,k,\varepsilon}
 (1+N)^k e^{-2\varepsilon N}.
\end{aligned}
 }
\tag{7.7}
\]

This proves absolute locally uniform convergence of all derivatives and
completes the contour argument.

## 8. Exact semilocal sign

For the mirror kernel \(K_h=C_{h,\mathsf Jh}\) and \(t=2y>1\), the frozen
pole--prime cancellation gives

\[
 \mathfrak q[S_yh]-\mathfrak q[h]
 =
 A_h(y)+e^{-y}M_{h,-}^2-J(2y),
\tag{8.1}
\]

where \(A_h(y)\) is the archimedean cross term. Therefore (7.1) yields

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
 \mathfrak q[S_yh]-\mathfrak q[h]
 ={}&
 A_h(y)+e^{-y}M_{h,-}^2\\
 &+
 \sum_\rho
 m_\rho H\!\left(\rho-\frac12\right)
 e^{(2\rho-1)y}\\
 &+
 \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}
 H\!\left(-2n-\frac12\right)
 e^{-(4n+1)y},
 \qquad y>\frac12.
\end{aligned}
 }
\tag{8.2}
\]

Thus:

- the error convolution \(J\) carries the leading algebraic minus signs in
  (7.1), without a positivity assertion for complex coefficients;
- the semilocal combined pole--prime contribution \(-J\) has the opposite
  algebraic sign displayed in (8.2), without asserting positivity of the
  generally complex nontrivial-zero coefficients;
- the main \(e^{y}\) pole term is absent because it was canceled at
  \(z=1/2\) before inversion.

Formula (8.2) is an unconditional smoothed explicit formula. It uses the
complete zero multiset of \(\zeta\) only to evaluate the already-defined
arithmetic interaction. It is not a compactness theorem and cannot be used
as hidden input for RH, zero completeness, or ground-state escape.

## 9. Conclusion

The canonical zero-extended full-line convolution (2.1) has the exact
bilateral transform (4.2), the rigorous inverse formula (5.2), and the
absolutely convergent zero expansion (7.1) for \(t>1\). The physical
interaction at \(t=2y\), \(y>1/2\), is independent of all choices below the
logarithmic endpoint \(r=0\), although the bilateral transform of an
arbitrary noncanonical extension need not be independent. The lower
endpoint, pole cancellation, nontrivial-zero residues, trivial-zero
residues, and semilocal sign are all fixed explicitly.
